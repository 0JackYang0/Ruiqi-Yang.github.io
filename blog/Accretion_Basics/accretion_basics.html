1. Introduction

Accretion disks exist in many scales: millions of kilometers in low mass X-ray binaries, Active galactic nuclei, cataclysmic variables, protostellars, protoplanets, etc.

Accretion disks, jets and outflows are observational connected. Lacking direct observations of disks, theory has tried to provide models. with varying degrees of success. Uncertainty still exists with respect to some basic questions. In this situation, progress made by observations or modeling of a particular class of objects has direct impact on the understanding of other objects, including the enigmatic connection with jets.

2. Accretion: general

Gas falling into a point mass potential \begin{equation} \Phi = - \frac{GM}{r} \label{eq:1.1} \end{equation} from a distance \(r_0\) to a distance \(r\) converts gravitational into kinetic energy by an amount \(\Delta\Phi=GM(1/r-1/r_0)\). Assuming that the starting distance is large, \(\Delta\Phi=GM/r\). The speed of arrival, the free-fall speed \(v_{ff}\), is \begin{equation} \frac12 v^2_{ff}=\frac{GM}{r} \label{eq:1.2} \end{equation}

If the gas is then brought to rest, the amount of energy \(e\) dissipated per unit mass is \begin{equation} e=\frac12 v^2_{ff}=\frac{GM}{r} \label{eq:1.3} \end{equation}

If the gas goes into a circular Kepler orbit at distance \(r\) \begin{equation} e=\frac12\frac{GM}{r} \label{eq:1.4} \end{equation}

The dissipated energy may go into internal energy of the gas, and into radiation, which escapes to infinity (usually in the form of photons, but neutrino losses are also significant in some cases)

2.1 Adiabatic accretion

Any mechanical energy dissipated stays locally in the flow, which is called an adiabatic flow. For an ideal gas with constant ratio of specific heats \(\gamma\), the internal energy per unit mass is \begin{equation} e=\frac{P}{(\gamma-1)\rho} \label{eq:1.5} \end{equation}

With Equation of State (EoS) \begin{equation} P=\frac{\mathcal{R}\rho T}{\mu} \label{eq:1.6} \end{equation} where \(\mathcal{R}\) is the gas constant and \(\mu\) is the mean atomic weight per particle.
